About This Build

by Roy Giorgio

Why I’m Building an Airplane

When I was a kid, my brother Skip took me up in his homebuilt EAA Biplane. The moment the wheels left the ground, I felt something I’ve never forgotten. I want to feel that same sense of elation when I fly a plane that I built with my own hands.

Of course, there are some more “practical” motivations too. Renting planes can be a pain — fleets change, checkouts multiply, and sometimes the airplane you reserved gets grounded for maintenance or worse, dented by the last pilot. And while there are plenty of talented A&Ps out there, I’ve also had experiences that make me want to be responsible for my own aircraft. Once, a Civil Air Patrol C172 went into the shop three times for a fuel issue. Each time it came back with “no problem found.” On the third attempt, I embarrassedly giggled when the engine nearly shook itself apart during a mag check. Another shop finally discovered a defect in the fuel servo that had been there since the airplane left the factory — and said they’d never seen spark plugs so fouled from constantly running rich. That experience cemented my desire to build and maintain my own airplane.

Choosing the Europa

I’ve looked at several aircraft over the years — even started building a KR2 Stretch and bought plans for a Tandem Airbike. The KR was simple and advertised as fast, but reality didn’t match the hype. After visiting KR gatherings, I learned most builders didn’t hit the published numbers, usually due to design deviations (or, let’s say, “creative craftsmanship”). The KR-2S plans also felt like juggling three different instruction manuals, and after fixing my third mistake before even finishing, I decided my project wasn’t airworthy.

The Tandem Airbike had potential, but the plans were more like loose suggestions. The metal airframe was no longer available, which didn’t help.

As retirement neared, I revisited my lifelong dream of building an airplane — this time with clear objectives: two seats, at least 125 knots cruise, a 500-pound useful load, less than 6 GPH at cruise, and folding or removable wings. I loved the Arion Lightning (built right here in Tennessee), but it lacked folding wings. Hangar space is scarce and expensive, and I refuse to keep an airplane outside. The Kitfox Speedster was appealing but a little slow — and had a 14-month waiting list.

Just for grins, I browsed Barnstormers and saw an unfinished Europa XS listed for about one-third the cost of a new kit. It felt like fate. Even better, it was only an hour from where I’d be in a few weeks. The work already done was meticulous, and the Europa checked every box on my list. So, I guess you could say this project is a happy collision of research, timing, and a dash of serendipity.

The Build Philosophy

I’m taking a slightly different approach than most builders. Too often, I see aircraft finished in a rush — fiberglass that looks like it was applied with duct tape, or paint that’s clearly fighting gravity. While looks don’t guarantee airworthiness, they do inspire confidence. A sloppy finish can make people wonder, “If it looks like that on the outside, what’s it like inside?”

That’s why I’m tackling much of the fiberglass filling and finishing early, before diving deep into the mechanical systems. I’m following the manual, but adding my finishing work as I go. As of this writing, I’ve logged about 75 hours of finish work. It’s easy to see how builders could lose patience near the end, but I’m determined not to cut corners.

Even though my kit’s early work looks great, I take nothing for granted. I’m verifying everything and completing all the recommended modifications. I’ve found two small errors so far — you can see where these are documented in the Trim Tab and Right Horizontal Stabilizer sections.

My plan is to use an Aeromomentum engine — based on a modern automotive block and fuel injected with an ECU. They’ve built airboat engines for decades and branched into aviation about ten years ago. I like that it’s a well-thought-out conversion, not just a car engine with a propeller stuck on it. Honestly, who’s had an injection issue on a car in the last 30 years? Meanwhile, I’ve had plenty of “fun” with aircraft engines that were supposedly bulletproof.

All control surfaces and systems will be built exactly per plans. Ivan Shaw knew what he was doing, and I have no interest in redesigning an airplane that already flies beautifully. There are definitely days when progress feels slow, but as long as there’s forward movement — even while waiting for epoxy to cure — I’m happy.

About Roy

I spent my career in broadcasting and later in communications, working alongside some remarkable engineers and creative teams. I’ve always loved solving problems — whether it was keeping a television station on the air or figuring out how to make a landing gear bracket fit perfectly. Building this airplane combines everything I enjoy: problem-solving, craftsmanship, and a bit of storytelling along the way.

My wife, Karen, deserves special recognition for her patience — and her tolerance of the noises, smells, and dust coming from the garage. She’s learned that if something smells funny or sounds like a jet engine in the driveway, it probably means progress.
